# Phase 3 — Mathematician Intuition

**Duration:** 6+ months. Open-ended. This is no longer about "finishing topics" — it's about developing **mathematical taste**.

**Goal:** Think like a mathematician. Read proofs and judge them. Write proofs and not be embarrassed. Develop intuition for *why* a theorem is true before grinding through the proof. Topology, real analysis, abstract algebra — the three legs of the stool.

---

## The shift in this phase

In Phase 2 you were a *user* of math — you formulated and solved problems. In Phase 3 you become an *understander*. You stop asking "how do I compute X" and start asking "why is X true" and "what makes X interesting."

This requires a different study mode:
- **Read slowly.** A page an hour is normal. A paragraph an hour is sometimes correct.
- **Pen always in hand.** Re-derive every example. Try every proof yourself before reading it.
- **Exercises are mandatory.** A chapter without exercises is an Instagram reel about math.
- **It will feel slow.** It is slow. That's the work.

Don't try all three tracks at once. Pick one, go deep for a couple months, then pick another. Topology is your stated interest — start there.

## Track 1 — Real Analysis (the bridge to rigour)

**Why first:** Analysis is where you learn what a *proof actually is*. ε-δ. Quantifier order. The painstaking honesty of "for every x there exists a δ such that..." This rewires your brain.

**Primary text:** Stephen Abbott, *Understanding Analysis* (2nd edition).
- Written for exactly your situation — strong-but-rusty student. The friendliest serious analysis book in existence.
- Aim: 6–10 weeks. One chapter per 7–10 days.

**Topics you'll cover:**
- Real numbers as a *complete ordered field*.
- Sequences, limits, Cauchy convergence.
- ε-δ definition of continuity.
- Differentiation (now with proofs, not just rules).
- Riemann integration — actual definition.
- Sequences of functions, uniform convergence.

**If you fall in love:** Rudin, *Principles of Mathematical Analysis* (the classic "baby Rudin"). Terse, beautiful, hard. Recommended *after* Abbott.

**Self-test:** Prove that a continuous function on a closed interval attains its maximum. From scratch. On paper.

## Track 2 — Topology (your stated interest)

**Step 1: Build intuition first (2 weeks).**
- **Jeffrey Weeks, *The Shape of Space*** — readable, beautiful, almost no prerequisites. Read it cover to cover before any rigorous text. It gives you the *pictures* that everything else will hang on.

**Step 2: General topology, rigorously (2–3 months).**
- **Armstrong, *Basic Topology*** — gentle and visual.
- *or* **Munkres, *Topology*** — the standard. More austere, more comprehensive. Either is fine; Munkres is the one most mathematicians own.

**Topics:**
- Topological spaces, open/closed sets.
- Continuity, homeomorphism (the *real* meaning of "two shapes are the same").
- Compactness — the most useful topological property. Why every continuous function on a compact set is bounded.
- Connectedness, path-connectedness.
- Quotient spaces (a torus = a square with opposite sides identified — internalize this).
- Metric spaces as a special case.

**Step 3: Algebraic topology (if you're hooked, 3+ months).**
- **Hatcher, *Algebraic Topology*** — free PDF from author's website.
- The fundamental group π₁. The intuition: it counts loops up to deformation.
- Homology, cohomology.
- The classification of surfaces — one of the prettiest results you'll meet.

**Step 4: Differential topology / geometry (if you go further).**
- Pressley, *Elementary Differential Geometry* (the surface-y, hands-on intro).
- Eventually Lee, *Introduction to Smooth Manifolds*.

**Self-test (general topology):** Prove that the image of a connected set under a continuous map is connected.

**Self-test (algebraic topology):** Explain to a non-mathematician why a coffee mug and a donut are "the same" — and what *exactly* "the same" means.

## Track 3 — Abstract Algebra

**Primary text:** Pinter, *A Book of Abstract Algebra* — story-driven, gentle, perfect for a self-learner. Cheap as a Dover paperback.

**Topics:**
- Groups: definition, examples (symmetric, cyclic, dihedral, matrix groups).
- Subgroups, cosets, Lagrange's theorem.
- Normal subgroups, quotients.
- Homomorphisms, isomorphism theorems.
- Rings, fields (lighter touch — full ring theory is for later).
- Galois theory glimpse (Pinter has a beautiful introduction).

**Why this matters:** Algebra trains you to think *structurally* — to see the same pattern in completely different objects (the integers mod n, rotations of a polygon, permutations of a set, all are groups). This abstraction is the mathematician's superpower.

**After Pinter:** Dummit & Foote, *Abstract Algebra* (the encyclopedia). Don't read cover-to-cover; treat as a reference.

**Self-test:** Prove Lagrange's theorem: the order of a subgroup divides the order of the group. (One of the most elegant short proofs in algebra.)

## How to study at this level — concrete habits

1. **The "proof attempt" ritual:**
   - Read the statement of a theorem.
   - Close the book. Try to prove it. Spend at least 20 minutes.
   - Open the book. Compare your attempt to the actual proof.
   - Note: your attempt failing is *good* — it shows you what you didn't yet see.

2. **The "example zoo":**
   - For every definition, collect 3 examples and 1 non-example.
   - "Compact space" — examples: [0,1], any finite set, the Cantor set. Non-example: ℝ.
   - Keep these in a notebook. It will become your most valuable possession.

3. **The "explain it back" ritual:**
   - After each chapter, explain its main result to a rubber duck / imaginary undergrad / actual friend.
   - If you can't, you haven't understood it yet.

4. **Study groups:**
   - Math is harder alone. Find one online: r/math, math.stackexchange, Discord servers around the textbook you're reading.
   - Even one weekly chat partner doubles your retention.

5. **The pen is more important than the screen.**
   - Hand-write proofs. The motor act of writing is part of how mathematicians think. PDFs and tablets are convenient, but a notebook is better for *deep* work.

## Exit criteria for Phase 3

There's no clean exit from this phase — math research is infinite. But signals that you've internalized the mathematician's mode of thinking:

- [ ] You finish Abbott (or equivalent) and can prove standard analysis facts from scratch.
- [ ] You finish at least one of Armstrong / Munkres / Hatcher and can explain compactness, connectedness, and the fundamental group fluently.
- [ ] You finish Pinter and can produce examples of groups and prove basic structural facts.
- [ ] You can read an undergraduate-level math paper or expository article on a *new* topic and follow its proofs.
- [ ] You have *taste*. When you see a theorem, you have an opinion about whether it's a deep result or a routine one.

At this point you have something most working programmers will never have: the ability to *think* in the language of mathematics. Move to [Phase 4 — Specialization](PHASE-4-specialization.md) when you're ready to apply this hard-won general fluency to specific domains.
