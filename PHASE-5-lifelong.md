# Phase 5 — Lifelong Practice

**Duration:** The rest of your life.

**Goal:** Keep math sharp. Keep getting better. Math fluency is like physical fitness — it atrophies if you stop. The good news: maintenance takes much less effort than acquisition.

---

## The daily / weekly / monthly routine

### Daily (10 minutes minimum)
- **Arithmetic drill.** Still. Forever. Use `lessons/arithmetic-drill.html`. Just 30 problems. It is the *single most important habit* in this whole curriculum.

### Weekly (30 minutes)
- **One Project Euler problem** OR one textbook exercise OR one self-set proof.
- The point is to *produce* something — solve, prove, derive. Passive consumption doesn't count.

### Monthly (a few hours, anytime in the month)
- **Read one math-adjacent paper.** Doesn't have to be deep. Skim a NeurIPS optimization paper. Read a Quanta article and then chase one citation. Read a section of a textbook you wouldn't otherwise touch.
- **Re-derive one foundational result from memory.** Rotate through: chain rule, Bayes' theorem, Lagrange multipliers, SVD existence, Cauchy-Schwarz, fundamental theorem of calculus, Lagrange's theorem (group theory). If you can't, you've found your next month's review.

### Quarterly (a weekend)
- **Teach something.** Add a lesson to this repo. Write a blog post. Explain to a friend. Answer a question on math.stackexchange. Teaching exposes the gaps no amount of solo study will.

### Annually
- **Audit yourself.** Pick a random page from each Phase's textbooks. Can you still do the exercises? If not, what topic decayed? Block out a week to revisit.

## The Project Euler track, long-term

Project Euler problems are *perfect* lifelong practice. They:
- Combine math and code.
- Force you to formalize.
- Scale with your level (problem 700 will challenge you even with Phase 4 math behind you).
- Are bite-sized — solvable in an evening once you're warmed up.

A pace of one a week gets you ~50 a year, which is excellent. The first 200 problems are doable; problems 200–700 require real ingenuity; 700+ are research-flavor.

## How to keep curiosity alive

Math, like any pursuit, dies when it becomes a chore. Some sustaining practices:

1. **Follow mathematicians on the internet.** Tao's blog (terrytao.wordpress.com), Quanta Magazine, n-Lab for the abstract types, 3Blue1Brown for the visual ones. Aperiodical, Mathologer. Subscribe.

2. **Go to math events.** Public lectures at a nearby university. Math meetups. The Joint Mathematics Meetings (largest annual gathering) is open to anyone — go once for the experience.

3. **Buy real books, not PDFs.** A bookshelf of math books you've actually worked through is a quiet daily reminder of who you are now.

4. **Keep a "math journal."** A physical notebook where, when you encounter an idea that grabs you, you spend 20 minutes with it. It accretes over years into something extraordinary.

5. **Don't compete on speed.** You're not racing anyone. The mathematician's pace is slow and that's fine. Reading 10 pages of Hatcher in a week is *real progress*.

6. **Take long walks while thinking about a problem.** This is what mathematicians actually do, far more than any other activity. The unconscious is doing math while you walk. Trust it.

## When you get stuck (you will)

Every working mathematician has periods where they're stuck. So will you. Some moves that help:

- **Switch tracks for a week.** If analysis is grinding you down, do topology. The break is restorative.
- **Re-read the last chapter that felt easy.** Often you've stacked too many new ideas without enough rest.
- **Talk to someone.** Even explaining your confusion out loud, to anyone, helps.
- **Sleep on it.** Genuinely. The "wake up with the answer" experience is real and common.
- **Take a month off.** Math will be here when you come back. Burnout is the only thing that ends this journey.

---

## A note on identity

Around Phase 2 or 3, you'll catch yourself describing yourself as "good at math" without flinching. That's a real shift — it took years to undo, it'll take years to redo. Honor it. The 17-year-old who liked math wasn't wrong about what kind of person you are; they were right, and time just intervened. You're getting that person back, plus everything you've learned since.

Keep going. The work is the reward.
