Why this matters at work and at Google

Every dashboard you read, every metric you ship, every "growth is X%" claim — it's percentages and growth rates. And the same math (exponential vs polynomial growth) is the intuition behind Big-O. So one lesson, two payoffs: become fast and right with percentages, then see how the same shapes power algorithm-complexity thinking.

The four percentage traps

1. "% of" vs "% change." 50% of 200 is 100. But going from 200 → 100 is a −50% change, while going from 100 → 200 is a +100% change. The base matters.

2. Percentage points vs percent. Going from 5% to 7% is "2 percentage points" — but it's a 40% increase. Confusing these in a deck will get you publicly corrected.

3. Reversibility. A 50% loss is not undone by a 50% gain. (Down 100 → 50, then up 50% → 75.) You need a 100% gain to recover.

4. Compound vs simple growth. 10% per month for a year is not 120% growth — it's about 213% (since 1.1¹² ≈ 3.14).

The Rule of 72

Doubling time ≈ 72 / growth-rate-%. Memorize this.

  • Active users growing 6% per month? Doubles in 72/6 = 12 months.
  • Inflation at 3% a year? Prices double in 72/3 = 24 years.
  • Investment at 9% a year? Doubles in 72/9 = 8 years.

This is just ln(2) ≈ 0.69 in disguise. The "72" makes the division clean.

Bridge to Big-O

That exponential curve you just watched? It's why O(2ⁿ) algorithms are infamous. At n = 30, an O(2ⁿ) algorithm takes ~1 billion steps. At n = 60, ~1 quintillion. The shape that compounds your savings account also makes brute-force recursion useless on large inputs.

Conversely, O(log n) is the opposite shape — slow growth no matter how big n gets. Binary search a billion items? ~30 steps.

Order of growth, from best to worst (memorize the ordering):

O(1) < O(log n) < O(n) < O(n log n) < O(n²) < O(n³) < O(2ⁿ) < O(n!)
