From basis to span (the geometric flip)

Day 15 said "basis = vectors that span the space." Today: what does span mean, exactly?

A linear combination of vectors v₁, ..., vₖ is any expression c₁v₁ + c₂v₂ + ... + cₖvₖ for real numbers cᵢ. The span of those vectors is the set of all possible linear combinations — every point you can reach.

So "basis of ℝ²" means: a set of vectors whose span is all of ℝ², with no redundancy.

The shapes a span can take in 2D

  • No vectors: span = {0}. Just the origin.
  • One non-zero vector: span = a line through the origin.
  • Two parallel vectors: still just a line (the second adds nothing).
  • Two non-parallel vectors: all of ℝ². They form a basis.

In 3D it scales up: 1 vector → line; 2 independent → plane; 3 independent → all of ℝ³. The pattern continues.

Why this matters for ML

  • Feature design: if your features are linearly dependent, they don't add information — the model "sees" the same span. This is why correlated features can hurt regularized models.
  • Embeddings: word/user/item embeddings live in some span. The dimension of that span is the "effective" capacity of your model.
  • PCA: finds the directions along which your data's span is "thickest" — and projects onto a basis aligned to those.
  • Solving Ax = b (tomorrow's lesson): has a solution iff b is in the span of A's columns. That's literally the geometric meaning.
