From "arrows" to a real algebraic structure

Day 3 introduced vectors as arrows in 2D. Day 4 introduced matrices as transformations. Now: the structure those arrows live in — a vector space — and three ideas you'll use forever: linear independence, basis, dimension.

Why now: every ML model thinks in terms of "the feature space," "the latent space," "the embedding space." Those are vector spaces. The dimension of a space tells you how many independent parameters you have. The basis tells you what coordinates mean. Without these, you'll forever be reading ML papers as a tourist.

Three definitions

Vector space (informal): a set V where you can add vectors and multiply by scalars, and the usual rules hold (associativity, distributivity, zero vector exists, etc.). Examples: ℝ², ℝ³, ℝⁿ, the space of polynomials, the space of functions on [0,1]. Most ML lives in ℝⁿ for some n.

Linear independence: a set of vectors {v₁, v₂, ..., vₖ} is independent if no vector is a linear combination of the others. Equivalently: c₁v₁ + c₂v₂ + ... + cₖvₖ = 0 forces all cᵢ = 0.

Basis: a linearly independent set that spans the whole space — meaning every vector in V can be written uniquely as a linear combination of the basis vectors.

Dimension: the number of vectors in any basis of V. Doesn't depend on which basis you pick (theorem). ℝ² has dimension 2. ℝⁿ has dimension n.

The geometric picture in 2D

  • One vector spans a line through the origin. (Dimension 1.)
  • Two linearly independent vectors span all of ℝ². (Dimension 2.)
  • Two linearly dependent vectors span just a line — the second adds no new direction. (Dimension 1.)
  • Three vectors in ℝ² are always linearly dependent (you can't have 3 independent directions in a 2D space).
