Reading the picture

  • Each constraint is a straight line; the feasible side is shaded.
  • The feasible region (where all constraints overlap) is the dark teal polygon.
  • The orange dashed lines are level sets of the objective. They march in the direction of increase.
  • The LP optimum sits at a vertex of the polygon. (Fundamental theorem of LP.)
  • In integer mode, the green dots are the feasible lattice points. The MILP optimum is the one with the highest objective — often not the LP optimum rounded.

Try this: with default settings, LP optimum is at (2.4, 3.2) with objective 13.6. Flip integer mode — MILP optimum is at (3, 2) with objective 13. The gap is 0.6. Notice that (3, 2) is not what you'd get by simply rounding (2.4, 3.2). This is the whole reason MILP needs special algorithms — you can't just solve the LP and round.

Why this matters

Real-world decision problems live in MILP-shaped space: scheduling, routing, packing, assignment, facility location. The integers represent discrete choices ("build the warehouse or don't"; "assign worker to shift or don't"). The LP relaxation gives you a lower bound; the gap to the MILP optimum is sometimes small, sometimes huge.

Branch-and-bound, the standard MILP algorithm, works by solving LP relaxations of restricted problems — splitting on fractional variables. Modern solvers (Gurobi, CPLEX, HiGHS, CBC) are extraordinarily good at this. They routinely solve problems with millions of variables.
