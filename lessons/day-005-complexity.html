Why today, why this matters at Google

Big-O is the language of algorithm interviews. Every Google SWE round will probe your complexity analysis. But it's more than interview kabuki — it's the lens for "will this scale?" which is the question your whole career as a backend engineer turns on.

Yesterday you saw exponential vs polynomial vs linear growth as shapes on a graph. Today you map that same picture onto algorithms.

The definition (informally)

O(f(n)) means: as n grows large, the running time of the algorithm grows no faster than some constant times f(n). We ignore constants. We ignore lower-order terms. Only the asymptotic shape matters.

So an algorithm that takes 3n² + 50n + 1000 operations is O(n²). The 3 doesn't matter; the +50n is dominated; the constant +1000 disappears.

This is the upper bound view. There's also Θ (tight bound), Ω (lower bound), and o (strict upper). 95% of the time you say "O" and mean Θ.

Practical rule: at the scale of 10⁸ operations per second, you have ~1 sec budget. So O(n²) is fine up to n ≈ 10,000. O(n log n) is fine to n ≈ 10⁷. O(2ⁿ) is dead by n=30. O(n!) is dead by n=10.

How to read complexity from code (the SWE-interview move)

  1. One loop over n items? O(n).
  2. Nested loop, both over n? O(n²).
  3. Halving the input each step? O(log n). (Binary search, balanced trees.)
  4. Sorting? Always at least O(n log n) for comparison-based.
  5. Recursion that branches into two subproblems of half-size? Master theorem says O(n) or O(n log n) depending on per-level work.
  6. Recursion that branches into two full-size subproblems? O(2ⁿ). This is brute-force territory — memoize and you collapse it to O(n) or so.
