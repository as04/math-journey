The most important rule in calculus

Backpropagation is just the chain rule applied carefully. Every modern ML system runs on the chain rule. So this is the calculus rule you most need to deeply understand, not memorize.

Today: derive it from limits, see it visually, and connect it to its role in ML.

The statement

If f and g are functions and h(x) = f(g(x)), then:

h'(x) = f'(g(x)) · g'(x)

In words: "derivative of outer (at inner) times derivative of inner."

The Leibniz notation is even cleaner. If y = f(u) and u = g(x), then:

dy/dx = dy/du · du/dx

The "du" pretends to cancel like a fraction. Pretend-cancellation is a powerful intuition device, even though dy/dx isn't actually a fraction.

Why it's true (the intuitive derivation)

Look at the rate-of-change definition:

h'(x) = lim (f(g(x+h)) − f(g(x))) / h

Multiply numerator and denominator by (g(x+h) − g(x)):

= lim [ (f(g(x+h)) − f(g(x))) / (g(x+h) − g(x)) ] · [ (g(x+h) − g(x)) / h ]

As h → 0: g(x+h) → g(x), so the first bracket → f'(g(x)), and the second → g'(x). Done.

(This sketch hides some technicalities — what if g(x+h) − g(x) = 0? — that real analysis cleans up. The intuition is correct.)

Why this matters for backprop

A neural network is a composition of functions: y = f_n(f_{n−1}(...f_1(x))). Each layer is a function. The loss is a function of the final output.

To train, you need ∂loss/∂weight for every weight in every layer. By the chain rule, this is a product of local derivatives — one per layer between the loss and the weight. Backpropagation is just the algorithm that computes those chained products efficiently (reusing intermediate values).

Modern frameworks (PyTorch, JAX) do this automatically — but knowing what they're doing under the hood is the difference between a user and an engineer.
