Derive it. Don't memorize it.

Bayes' theorem is famously feared but completely trivial. From Day 9 you know two definitions:

P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)    and    P(B | A) = P(A ∩ B) / P(A)

Rearrange the second: P(A ∩ B) = P(B | A) · P(A). Substitute into the first:

P(A | B) = P(B | A) · P(A) / P(B)

That's Bayes' theorem. Nothing more. Derive it every time — within a week, you won't need to anymore.

The vocabulary (memorize)

  • P(A) — the prior. What you believed about A before seeing evidence B.
  • P(B | A) — the likelihood. How likely the evidence B is, assuming A is true.
  • P(B) — the marginal or evidence. How likely the evidence is overall.
  • P(A | B) — the posterior. Your updated belief about A after seeing B.

The whole game: Bayes converts likelihoods (which you can model: "if I had the disease, would I test positive?") into posteriors (what you actually want to know: "given a positive test, do I have the disease?"). Likelihoods are forward; posteriors are backward. Bayes flips them.

The disease test, properly

The classic example. A test for a rare disease:

  • Sensitivity (true-positive rate): 99%. If you have the disease, the test is positive 99% of the time.
  • Specificity (true-negative rate): 99%. If you don't have the disease, the test is negative 99% of the time.
  • Base rate (prevalence): 1%. 1 in 100 people have the disease.

You test positive. What's the probability you have the disease?

Most people, including doctors, guess ~99%. The actual answer is ~50%. Here's the Bayes computation:

P(D | +) = P(+ | D) · P(D) / P(+)
P(+) = P(+ | D)·P(D) + P(+ | ¬D)·P(¬D) = 0.99 · 0.01 + 0.01 · 0.99 = 0.0198
P(D | +) = (0.99 · 0.01) / 0.0198 = 0.5

The intuition: even a 1% false-positive rate produces a lot of false positives when the disease is rare. They drown the true positives.

Where this shows up at Google

  • A/B test significance interpretation: "p < 0.05" is P(data | null hypothesis), not P(null is true | data). Bayes-aware analysts don't confuse them.
  • Spam filters: classical naïve Bayes. P(spam | words) ∝ P(words | spam) · P(spam).
  • Recommender systems: P(user likes item | past behavior) — Bayesian posterior over preferences.
  • Anomaly detection: P(anomaly | observation). Base rate of anomalies is tiny, so naive thresholds drown in false positives. Same disease-test math.
