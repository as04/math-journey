A single number that tells you a lot

The determinant of a matrix is one number, but it tells you several things at once:

  1. Volume scaling. det(A) = how much the transformation scales volumes (areas in 2D, volumes in 3D, ...).
  2. Orientation. Negative det = orientation flipped (reflection).
  3. Invertibility. det = 0 ⟺ A is singular (no inverse). Non-zero det ⟺ A is invertible.
  4. Independence. det ≠ 0 ⟺ A's columns (or rows) are linearly independent.

The 2×2 formula (and why it's the area)

det([[a, b], [c, d]]) = ad − bc

Geometrically: the transformation takes the unit square to the parallelogram with sides (a, c) and (b, d). The signed area of that parallelogram is exactly ad − bc. Sign = orientation.

Properties to know

  • Multiplicativity: det(AB) = det(A) · det(B). Hugely useful — composing transformations multiplies their volume scalings.
  • Inverse: det(A⁻¹) = 1/det(A). (Inverting a transformation inverts its volume scaling.)
  • Transpose: det(Aᵀ) = det(A). The transpose doesn't change the determinant.
  • Triangular matrices: the determinant is just the product of diagonal entries. (Why LU decomposition makes computing det easy.)

Why this shows up in ML & stats

  • Change of variables in probability. When you transform a random variable Y = f(X), the density transforms with a factor of |det(Jacobian)|. The Jacobian is just the matrix of partials — and its determinant is the local volume scaling.
  • Multivariate Gaussians. The density has a det(Σ)⁻¹ᐟ² factor — measures how much "volume" the distribution occupies in space.
  • Normalizing flows (a generative-model family) literally track the log|det Jacobian| at each layer to maintain a valid probability distribution.
