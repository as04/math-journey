Why we need spread, not just average

Two bets, both with E[X] = $0:

  • Bet A: win $1, lose $1, each with probability 1/2.
  • Bet B: win $1,000,000, lose $1,000,000, each with probability 1/2.

Same expectation, completely different bets. Expectation alone is insufficient. We need a measure of spread — how far values typically deviate from the mean. That's variance.

Definitions

Var(X) = E[(X − μ)²]    where μ = E[X]

Average squared distance from the mean. Always ≥ 0; zero only if X is constant.

Two algebraic forms — both useful:

Var(X) = E[X²] − (E[X])²

The "expectation of the square minus square of the expectation" form. Often easier to compute.

The standard deviation is just the square root: σ = √Var(X). Same units as X (variance is in squared units). Use std for human-readable spread; variance is what the math operates on.

Variance rules (unlike expectation, has conditions)

  • Var(a·X + b) = a² · Var(X) — constants come out squared; shifts don't matter.
  • Var(X + Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y) + 2·Cov(X, Y)
  • If X, Y independent: Cov(X, Y) = 0, so Var(X + Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y).

The √n result: n independent samples of X. Sample mean has variance σ²/n. Std of the mean is σ/√n. This is why "more data" reduces uncertainty as the square root of n, not linearly. The reason your A/B test needs 4× the sample to halve the uncertainty.

Covariance and correlation

Cov(X, Y) = E[(X − μₓ)(Y − μᵧ)]

Positive cov: X and Y tend to move together. Negative: they move opposite. Zero: no linear relationship (but they could still be related nonlinearly).

Covariance has weird units (X-units × Y-units). To normalize, use correlation:

ρ(X, Y) = Cov(X, Y) / (σₓ · σᵧ)

Always in [−1, 1]. ρ = ±1 means perfect linear relationship. ρ = 0 means no linear relationship (different from independent).
