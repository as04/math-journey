The renormalization view

Conditional probability is the single most useful — and most misused — idea in applied probability. The whole game is captured in one formula:

P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)

Read aloud: "probability of A given B". Mental model: you've been told B happened, so throw away the rest of the sample space. Renormalize. Now, what fraction of B is also A?

Visual: two dice

Roll two dice. P(sum = 8) = 5/36 (favorable: (2,6),(3,5),(4,4),(5,3),(6,2)).

Now suppose someone tells you the first die was a 3. Your sample space shrinks from 36 to 6. The only sum-8 outcome consistent with first-die=3 is (3,5) — so P(sum=8 | first die = 3) = 1/6.

Computed via the formula: P(sum=8 AND first=3) = 1/36. P(first=3) = 6/36 = 1/6. So P(sum=8 | first=3) = (1/36) / (1/6) = 1/6. ✓

Two famous traps

Trap 1: P(A|B) ≠ P(B|A).

  • P(person is a basketball player | person is tall) is fairly high.
  • P(person is tall | person is a basketball player) is essentially 1.
  • These are very different numbers. Swapping them is the prosecutor's fallacy and underlies many bad statistical arguments in real life.

Trap 2: independence vs disjoint.

  • Disjoint A and B: P(A ∩ B) = 0. They can't both happen. (Like "die shows 3" and "die shows 5".)
  • Independent A and B: P(A ∩ B) = P(A)·P(B). Knowing one tells you nothing about the other. (Like "first die" and "second die".)
  • These are nearly opposite concepts, but they get conflated constantly. Disjoint events with positive probability are maximally dependent (one happening rules out the other).
