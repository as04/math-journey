Track C focus: the SWE staple

Sorting is the most-asked-about algorithm family in tech interviews. Not because anyone implements sort from scratch at work (you call sorted()), but because sorting is the cleanest playground for understanding divide and conquer, recursion, time/space tradeoffs, and the meaning of asymptotic bounds.

Watching different algorithms sort the same array makes Big-O visible. Bubble sort literally crawls. Mergesort halves the work. You'll feel the complexity in your bones.

In practice: Python's sorted() is Timsort (mergesort + insertion-sort hybrid). C++'s std::sort is introsort (quicksort + heapsort fallback). Both are O(n log n) and adaptive.

The two big ideas to internalize

1. Why all comparison-based sorts have a lower bound of Ω(n log n).

To sort n items, you must distinguish among n! possible orderings. Each comparison gives 1 bit. So you need at least log₂(n!) ≈ n log₂ n comparisons. No clever comparison sort beats this — it's a fundamental information-theoretic bound.

(Radix sort and counting sort sidestep this — they're not comparison-based; they assume something about the data, like bounded integer keys.)

2. Why mergesort wins on guarantees, quicksort wins on cache.

Mergesort is O(n log n) every time, but uses O(n) extra memory. Quicksort is O(n log n) on average but O(n²) worst case — yet it's faster in practice because it sorts in place, has great cache behavior, and worst-case is rare with random pivot choice. This is the canonical "asymptotic vs constant-factor" trade-off.
