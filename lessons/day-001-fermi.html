Why this is Day 1

The single most professionally useful math skill — more than calculus, more than linear algebra — is the ability to estimate an unknown number to within an order of magnitude. It's what lets you sanity-check a claim in a meeting, sniff out a bad metric on a dashboard, or judge whether a project is worth the engineering cost. Physicists call this a Fermi estimate, after Enrico Fermi, who could estimate almost anything in his head using a chain of educated guesses.

The mental move: decompose the unknown into multiplied factors you can guess. Round aggressively. Multiply. Don't be precise — be right within a factor of 10.

Classic example. "How many piano tuners in Chicago?" → (population of Chicago) × (fraction owning a piano) × (tunings per piano per year) ÷ (tunings a single tuner does per year). Each factor you can guess. Multiply them. You land around a few hundred. The real number: ~250.

Worked: weight of air in a room

How much does the air in an average bedroom (4m × 5m × 3m) weigh, in kilograms?

  • Volume: 4 × 5 × 3 = 60 m³.
  • Density of air: ~1.2 kg/m³ (worth memorizing).
  • Weight: 60 × 1.2 ≈ 72 kg.

That's the weight of a grown adult. Most people guess "a few kilos" — off by a factor of 30. Estimation finds the right order of magnitude even when raw intuition betrays you.
